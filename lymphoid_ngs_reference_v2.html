Lymphoid NGS Reference Guide

A consolidated practical reference for hematopathology sign-out, integrating core lymphoma mutation patterns, pathway-based interpretation, subtype associations, and newly added points from the reviewed NHL NGS paper.

Updated content includes: DLBCL molecular subtype frameworks (Chapuy / Schmitz / LymphGen), follicular lymphoma molecular prognostic models including m7-FLIPI and POD24 markers, mantle cell lymphoma genomic risk subsets, and liquid biopsy / ctDNA applications.

1. Overview

Next-generation sequencing (NGS) is increasingly integrated into lymphoma workup to support diagnosis, refine classification, improve prognostic assessment, and identify therapeutically relevant alterations. NGS should be interpreted together with morphology, immunophenotype, flow cytometry, cytogenetics/FISH, and clinical context.

Core principle: lymphoma NGS interpretation is usually about pattern recognition, not isolated mutation listing.

Practical use cases include:

  • Narrowing a difficult differential diagnosis
  • Supporting a specific lymphoma subtype
  • Recognizing molecular high-risk disease
  • Identifying pathway-based therapeutic relevance

Crosswalk Concept

Chapuy, Schmitz, and later LymphGen classifications overlap substantially. Practical sign-out does not require memorizing every published schema, but it is useful to recognize the major clusters because NGS reports increasingly use this language.

  • MCD roughly aligns with high-risk MYD88/CD79B-driven disease
  • BN2 aligns with NOTCH2/BCL6-fusion biology
  • EZB aligns with EZH2/BCL2-translocation GCB disease
  • TP53 / aneuploid groups identify adverse-risk cases
Sign-out pearl: when MYD88 L265P and CD79B coexist in a large B-cell lymphoma, that pattern is usually much more informative than either mutation alone.

m7-FLIPI

The m7-FLIPI integrates mutation data with clinical FLIPI parameters to improve risk stratification in patients treated with conventional immunochemotherapy. It may reclassify some clinically high-risk cases into a lower molecular-risk group, especially when EZH2 mutation is present.

Its performance appears weaker in some chemotherapy-free frontline settings.

AITL Pathogenetic Sequence Concept

In AITL, mutations such as TET2 and DNMT3A may occur early at the hematopoietic stem cell level, whereas lesions such as RHOA, VAV1, and CD28 act later in T-cell transformation.

10. Pathway-Based Interpretation

B-cell Receptor Signaling

  • CD79B
  • CARD11
  • BTK
  • PLCG2

Typical of activated B-cell–type large B-cell lymphomas.

NF-kB Signaling

  • MYD88
  • CARD11
  • TNFAIP3

Supports activated B-cell lymphoid biology and survival signaling.

Epigenetic Regulation

  • EZH2
  • KMT2D
  • CREBBP
  • EP300
  • TET2
  • DNMT3A

Prominent in FL, GCB-DLBCL, and several T-cell lymphomas.

JAK / STAT Signaling

  • STAT3
  • STAT5B
  • JAK1
  • JAK3

Important in T/NK neoplasms and selected aggressive lymphomas.

12. Stepwise Interpretation Algorithm

Step 1 — Look for near-diagnostic mutations

  • BRAF V600E
  • MYD88 L265P
  • RHOA G17V
  • STAT3 / STAT5B

Step 2 — Look for mutation patterns

  • MYD88 + CD79B
  • MYD88 + CXCR4
  • EZH2 + CREBBP + KMT2D
  • NOTCH2 + KLF2
  • RHOA + TET2 + IDH2

Step 3 — Ask which pathway is activated

BCR, NF-kB, epigenetic, PI3K, or JAK/STAT convergence often helps convert a mutation list into biologic meaning.

Step 4 — Identify adverse-risk lesions

Flag TP53, CDKN2A, MYC, B2M, and aggressive copy-number style patterns when available.

Step 5 — Correlate with morphology and phenotype

NGS supports but does not replace diagnostic morphology. Mutation profiles must fit the tissue findings.

13. Liquid Biopsy / ctDNA

Liquid biopsy is an important evolving area in lymphoma genomics. ctDNA may help in diagnosis, disease burden assessment, response monitoring, relapse detection, and minimal residual disease evaluation.

ApplicationPractical Takeaway
Initial assessmentctDNA burden can correlate with LDH, IPI, TMTV, and stage
Interim monitoringDynamic ctDNA decline can assess tumor kinetics during therapy
Early failure detectionMay detect relapse risk before imaging-based progression
Post-treatment surveillancePotential MRD tool, including after CAR-T therapy
Biologic heterogeneityMay capture broader tumor genomic landscape than a single tissue sample
Caution: ctDNA is highly promising, but validation and standardization remain incomplete for routine universal use across all lymphoma settings.

14. Example Reporting Language

Example 1

Targeted next-generation sequencing identified MYD88 L265P and CD79B mutations. This mutational profile is characteristic of the MCD molecular subtype of diffuse large B-cell lymphoma and is commonly encountered in primary CNS and testicular lymphomas. Correlation with morphologic and immunophenotypic findings is recommended.

Example 2

Targeted next-generation sequencing detected EZH2, KMT2D, and CREBBP mutations. In the appropriate morphologic and immunophenotypic setting, this profile supports a follicular lymphoma / germinal-center program.

Example 3

Targeted next-generation sequencing identified RHOA, TET2, and IDH2 mutations. This combination is highly characteristic of angioimmunoblastic T-cell lymphoma / TFH-type lymphoma, pending correlation with histologic and phenotypic findings.

15. Practical Pearls

  • Mutation combinations usually matter more than individual mutations.
  • MYD88 is helpful, but MYD88 + CD79B is far more specific for a DLBCL molecular subtype.
  • In FL, NGS adds real value by identifying transformation risk and molecular prognostic patterns.
  • In MCL, TP53 and CDKN2A immediately raise concern for aggressive behavior.
  • In T-cell lymphomas, RHOA + TET2 + IDH2 is one of the most useful recognizable patterns.
  • ctDNA is one of the most important evolving areas likely to affect lymphoma monitoring practice.
