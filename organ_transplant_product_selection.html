Transfusion in Organ Transplantation

Practical guide for blood component selection in solid organ transplantation, especially ABO-incompatible settings.

Solid organ transplantation

Core Principles

RBCs

Choose red cells that avoid exposing the recipient to incompatible donor-recipient ABO issues. In major ABO-incompatible solid organ transplantation, recipient-type RBCs are generally preferred; group O is the usual backup option.

Platelets

Platelets carry both cellular ABO antigens and plasma antibodies. ABO-identical platelets are biologically most desirable. Non-identical platelets may have lower increments or passive hemolytic risk, especially with group O platelets containing anti-A,B.

Plasma

Plasma choice should avoid antibodies directed against antigens expressed on the graft or recipient. In ABO-incompatible transplant settings, donor-type plasma is often preferred when compatible and when it avoids unnecessary use of group AB plasma.

General Practical Rules

  • Think separately about cellular products and plasma-rich products.
  • For RBCs, favor the product that is safest for the recipient and least likely to create hemolysis.
  • For plasma and platelets, avoid anti-A or anti-B directed against ABO antigens on the transplanted organ.
  • When both donor-type and AB plasma or platelets are acceptable, donor-type plasma-containing products may conserve group AB inventory and may be immunologically preferable in some major ABO-incompatible settings.
  • Use local transplant/transfusion service protocols for timing of switching back to conventional recipient-type support after transplant.

Why These Choices Make Sense

Recipient-type RBCs are preferred

Routine use of group O RBCs is not always necessary. The proposed guideline favors recipient-type RBCs first, with group O as backup in applicable settings.

Donor-type plasma-containing products may be better than default AB

For some major ABO-incompatible transplants, donor-type platelets or plasma are suggested ahead of AB when both are compatible, especially to minimize unnecessary deviation from donor ABO and preserve scarce AB plasma inventory.

Platelets are more complex

Platelet support involves both cellular ABO mismatch and plasma incompatibility. ABO-identical products are ideal when feasible, while major, minor, or bidirectional disparity may affect increment or hemolytic risk.

Other Transfusion Considerations in Organ Transplantation

  • RhD: usually not graft-relevant, but still important for alloimmunization prevention and HDFN-conscious practice in applicable patients.
  • HLA sensitization: relevant for transplantation and for platelet refractoriness; transplant and HLA laboratory input may be needed.
  • Passenger lymphocyte syndrome: can occur after solid organ transplantation and may cause hemolysis, especially in minor ABO-incompatible settings.
  • Product modifications: irradiation, CMV-safe support, and antigen-negative units may be required depending on the clinical scenario and local transplant protocols.

Cautions

This page is a practical summary. Exact peri-transplant and post-transplant product selection should follow your institution's transplant protocol and direct transfusion medicine consultation. Timing for continuing ABO non-identical support after transplant varies by center and by organ type.
