Transfusion in Organ Transplantation

Practical reference for blood component support in solid organ transplantation, with emphasis on ABO-incompatible settings, perioperative product selection, and common transfusion-related complications.

Solid organ transplantation

Core Principles

Red Blood Cells

For major ABO-incompatible solid organ transplantation, recipient-type RBCs are generally preferred first. Group O RBCs are the usual backup option when needed.

Platelets

Platelets carry both ABO antigens and donor plasma antibodies. ABO-identical platelets are biologically most desirable. Non-identical products may produce lower increments or passive hemolytic risk.

Plasma

Plasma should avoid anti-A or anti-B that could react with antigens on the graft or recipient. In ABO-incompatible settings, donor-type plasma may be preferred over default AB plasma in selected situations.

General Practical Rules

  • Think separately about cellular products and plasma-rich products.
  • For RBCs, favor the product safest for the recipient and least likely to cause hemolysis.
  • For plasma and platelets, avoid anti-A or anti-B directed against ABO antigens expressed on the transplanted organ.
  • When donor-type and AB plasma-containing products are both acceptable, donor-type products may conserve AB inventory and may be immunologically preferable in some major ABO-incompatible settings.
  • Use local transplant and transfusion service protocols for when to transition back to routine recipient-type support after transplant.

Why These Choices Make Sense

Recipient-type RBCs are preferred

Routine use of group O RBCs is not always necessary. Recipient-type RBCs are often the preferred first choice, with group O reserved as backup where applicable.

Donor-type plasma may be better than default AB

When both donor-type and AB plasma-containing products are compatible, donor-type support may better preserve AB inventory and may reduce immunologic disadvantages associated with universal AB plasma.

Platelets are more complex

Platelet support involves both cellular ABO disparity and plasma incompatibility. ABO-identical units are ideal when feasible, but disparity may alter increment and hemolytic risk.

Passenger Lymphocyte Syndrome

  • Definition: donor lymphocytes transferred with the graft produce antibodies against recipient RBCs, leading to immune hemolysis.
  • Typical timing: about 7 to 14 days after transplant, but can range from 5 to 21 days.
  • Risk setting: especially minor ABO mismatch, such as O donor to A recipient.
  • Clues: falling hemoglobin, indirect hyperbilirubinemia, positive DAT, and donor-type antibodies in recipient plasma.
  • Management: usually supportive, including donor-compatible RBC transfusion; severe cases may require escalation of immunosuppression, plasmapheresis, or other targeted therapy.

Desensitization and Bleeding Risk

  • ABO-incompatible transplant recipients may have increased postoperative bleeding after plasmapheresis or immunoadsorption.
  • Mechanisms include thrombocytopenia, depletion of coagulation factors including fibrinogen and factor XIII, and citrate-related electrolyte disturbances.
  • Bleeding risk tends to increase with the number of pre-transplant apheresis sessions.
  • Mitigation may include factor replacement, careful platelet support, and correction of calcium, potassium, and magnesium abnormalities.

Other Transfusion Considerations

RhD

RhD is usually not graft-relevant, but remains important for alloimmunization prevention and HDFN-conscious practice in applicable patients.

HLA sensitization

HLA sensitization matters for transplantation and platelet refractoriness. HLA laboratory input may be required in selected cases.

Product modifications

CMV-safe support is relevant for selected solid organ transplant recipients. Irradiation is not routine for most solid organ transplantation, but may be indicated in special circumstances.

Timing of Product Transition

  • Continue donor-compatible products through the immediate perioperative and early post-transplant phase as dictated by protocol.
  • Monitor isoagglutinin titers and clinical course before transitioning back to routine support.
  • Remain alert for passenger lymphocyte syndrome during the 1 to 3 week post-transplant window.
  • Exact timing varies by organ type and by institutional protocol.

Cautions

This page is a practical summary rather than a universal protocol. Exact peri-transplant and post-transplant product selection should follow local transplant policy and direct transfusion medicine consultation, especially for ABO-incompatible cases.
