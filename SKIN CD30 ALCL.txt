Primary Cutaneous Anaplastic Large Cell Lymphoma (PCALCL)
DIAGNOSIS:
Skin, Left Arm, Biopsy:
Primary Cutaneous Anaplastic Large Cell Lymphoma (PCALCL)
See comment

COMMENT:
The histologic and immunohistochemical findings are consistent with aĀprimary cutaneous anaplastic large cell lymphoma (PCALCL). The neoplastic cells demonstrate strong CD30 positivity and a T-cell/null-cell phenotype, with loss of multiple T-cell markers (CD2, CD3, CD4, CD5, CD7, and CD8). The tumor cells are positive for CD45, granzyme B, perforin, bcl-2, EMA, and Ki67, and negative for ALK-1, CD20, and EBER. The absence of ALK expression supports a primary cutaneous origin, as systemic ALCL is typically ALK-positive. However, EMA positivity raises the possibility of secondary cutaneous involvement by systemic ALCL, and further work-up (e.g., imaging, bone marrow biopsy) is recommended to exclude systemic disease.

MICROSCOPIC DESCRIPTION:
Histologic sections of the skin biopsy show a diffuse dermal infiltrate of predominantly large, anaplastic cells with vesicular nuclei, irregular nuclear contours, and prominent nucleoli. Scattered hallmark cells with horseshoe-shaped nuclei are present. The tumor cells are admixed with a background of neutrophils, small lymphocytes, and occasional plasma cells. Mitotic figures are readily identified. Epidermotropism is minimal, and there is no evidence of ulceration.

IMMUNOHISTOCHEMISTRY:
The neoplastic cells are positive for CD30, CD45, granzyme B, perforin, bcl-2, EMA, and Ki67. They are negative for CD1a, CD2, CD3, CD4, CD5, CD7, CD8, CD10, CD20, CD34, CD56, Bcl-6, ALK-1, and EBER. TIA1 is equivocal due to suboptimal staining.

CLINICAL CORRELATION:
The findings are consistent with PCALCL. Clinical correlation with the presence of solitary or localized skin lesions is recommended. Further work-up, including imaging and bone marrow biopsy, is suggested to rule out systemic involvement.

COMMENT:
The histologic and immunohistochemical findings are consistent withĀlymphomatoid papulosis (LyP), Type A. The neoplastic cells demonstrate strong CD30 positivity and a T-cell phenotype, with expression of CD4 and cytotoxic markers (granzyme B, perforin). The polymorphic background infiltrate, including eosinophils and neutrophils, is characteristic of LyP. The clinical history of recurrent, self-regressing papules further supports this diagnosis.

MICROSCOPIC DESCRIPTION:
Histologic sections of the skin biopsy show a mixed dermal infiltrate of large, atypical CD30-positive cells admixed with small lymphocytes, eosinophils, neutrophils, and histiocytes. The large cells have irregular nuclear contours, vesicular chromatin, and prominent nucleoli. Scattered hallmark cells with horseshoe-shaped nuclei are present. Epidermotropism is minimal, and there is no evidence of ulceration.

IMMUNOHISTOCHEMISTRY:
The neoplastic cells are positive for CD30, CD4, CD45, granzyme B, perforin, and Ki67. They are negative for CD1a, CD2, CD3, CD5, CD7, CD8, CD10, CD20, CD34, CD56, Bcl-6, ALK-1, and EBER. TIA1 is equivocal due to suboptimal staining.

CLINICAL CORRELATION:
The findings are consistent with lymphomatoid papulosis (LyP), Type A. Clinical correlation with the presence of recurrent, self-regressing papules is recommended. Long-term follow-up is advised, as LyP is associated with an increased risk of secondary lymphomas.

1,2. SKIN, LEFT HIP ANTERIOR AND POSTERIOR:
H&E-stained sections of parts 1 and 2 show punch biopsies of skin containing an atypical lymphoid infiltrate within the dermis, featuring abundant atypical large anaplastic cells with irregular nuclear contours, variably condensed chromatin with prominent nucleoli and eosinophilic cytoplasm. Small mature lymphocytes, histiocytes and scattered eosinophils are admixed in this infiltrate. The epidermis is uninvolved, however in part 2 there is a band-like proliferation of small mature lymphocytes within the superficial dermis overlying the anaplastic infiltrate. These lymphocytes are small and mature, fairly monomorphic in appearance but with no other significant cytologic atypia. The unoriented lateral margins are involved in both biopsies.

Immunostains performed at BC Cancer on block 2A show the atypical large cells are positive for CD30(strong diffuse), CD2, CD4, MUM1 and BCL6, while negative for CD3, CD5, CD20, PAX5, EBER(ish) and ALK1. The band-like area of small lymphocytes contains predominantly CD20+ PAX5+ B-cells which express CD23, IgD and BCL2, and also show partial weak staining for CD5 in scattered cells. They are negative for CD10, BCL6, and Cyclin D1. Kappa and lambda immunostains do not uncover any light chain restriction among B-cells or plasma cells. A CD21 immunostain is negative for follicular dendritic cell meshworks. A Ki67 immunostain shows the great majority of large anaplastic cells to be in cycle, and is low (5%) in the small B-cells.


3. SKIN, LEFT BUTTOCK (SHAVE BIOPSY):

H&E-stained sections of part 3 show a shave biopsy of skin with mild perivascular lymphocytic inflammation. Lymphocytes are small and show no significant atypia. The superficial dermis shows fibrosis and sclerosis. The epidermis features mild acanthosis and hyperkeratosis, with only very focal exocytosis of small lymphocytes. No eosinophils are seen. Atypical large lymphocytes are not present.

Immunostains performed at BC Cancer on block 3A show lymphocytes are CD3+ T-cells with a mix of CD4+ and CD8+ forms and no abnormal loss of CD2, CD5 or CD7. A PAS-D special stain is negative for fungal elements.


In summary, parts 1 and 2 show involvement by a CD30+ T-cell lymphoma morphologically and immunophenotypically similar to previous specimens, compatible with progression/recurrence of the previously diagnosed primary cutaneous anaplastic large cell lymphoma. Additionally, part 2 shows an unusual lichenoid B-cell proliferation overlying the tumor. Although clonality testing on a previous biopsy (BC24-989) showed the B-cells admixed within the T-cell lymphoma to be non-clonal, given the atypical pattern in this biopsy, repeat B-cell clonality testing as well as send-out immunohistochemistry is pending to rule out a composite low-grade B-cell lymphoproliferative disorder. Results will be reported in an addendum. Part 3 shows features suggestive of a chronic spongiotic dermatitis, with no histologic evidence of involvement by cutaneous T-cell lymphoma.

Correlation with clinical/imaging findings is recommended for full evaluation, and to rule out the possibility of extracutaneous disease.
