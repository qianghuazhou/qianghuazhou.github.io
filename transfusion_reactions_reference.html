Transfusion Reactions

Reference page for recognition, immediate management, investigation, classification, reporting, and selected major transfusion reactions in routine transfusion medicine practice.

Overview

This page summarizes a practical clinical approach to transfusion reactions, including immediate bedside actions, basic and expanded laboratory workup, major acute and delayed reaction patterns, and Canadian reporting pathways. The emphasis is on a structured clinical reference rather than exam-style review.

Immediate Approach

Immediate bedside steps

  • Stop the transfusion immediately.
  • Maintain IV access.
  • Assess the patient and check vital signs, including ABCs and oxygenation.
  • Perform clerical checks, including patient identification, sample identification, and product label verification.
  • Notify the responsible physician so the patient can be assessed clinically.
  • Notify the blood bank / transfusion service.
  • Complete required paperwork and send appropriate post-transfusion samples if indicated.
A suspected acute hemolytic or septic reaction should be treated as urgent. In addition to investigating the current patient, consider whether another patient may have received the wrong component or be at risk from a companion error.

Patient monitoring during transfusion

  • Baseline vital signs before transfusion.
  • Repeat observations after 15 minutes at the slow initial rate.
  • Vital signs at completion of transfusion.
  • Additional observations at any time if there is concern for a reaction.
  • Ongoing hourly monitoring during transfusion.
  • Final set of observations 15 minutes after the unit is completed.

When cautious continuation may be acceptable

  • Minor allergic reaction with limited urticaria / pruritus and no other symptoms.
  • Simple febrile non-hemolytic reaction in a stable patient without concerning associated features.
Institutional policy may vary. Any hypotension, dyspnea, back pain, rigors, hemoglobinuria, worsening fever, or systemic deterioration should prompt discontinuation and full investigation.

Initial Laboratory Investigation

Basic elements of transfusion reaction testing

  • Clerical check
  • Visual inspection for hemolysis
  • Repeat ABO check
  • Direct antiglobulin test (DAT)

Assessment for free hemoglobin / hemoglobinemia

  • Visual inspection of centrifuged plasma for discoloration
  • Direct spectrophotometric plasma free hemoglobin measurement
  • Peroxidase-based methods
  • Other analytic methods such as mass spectrometry in specialized settings

Visual inspection of plasma is rapid and sensitive to low levels of hemolysis, although it is not specific.

Tier 1

  • Clerical check
  • Post-transfusion blood sample for hemoglobinemia
  • DAT
  • Repeat ABO typing

Tier 2

  • Peripheral smear
  • Bilirubin, LDH, haptoglobin
  • Urine hemoglobin
  • Coagulation testing
  • Renal function and electrolytes
  • Repeat serology on unit and pre/post samples as needed

Tier 3

  • Expanded antibody investigation
  • Eluate studies where appropriate
  • Donor unit investigation / phenotyping
  • Repeat antibody screen and AHG crossmatch using alternative methods if needed
  • Review transfusion technique and storage conditions

Reaction Classification

Acute reactions

Acute transfusion reactions are generally defined as reactions occurring within 24 hours of transfusion.

  • Allergic reaction
  • Anaphylactic reaction
  • Febrile non-hemolytic transfusion reaction (FNHTR)
  • Acute hemolytic transfusion reaction (AHTR)
  • TRALI
  • Septic / bacterial contamination
  • TACO
  • Hypotensive transfusion reaction
  • Non-immune hemolysis
  • Other product- or procedure-related reactions

Delayed reactions

Delayed transfusion reactions are typically defined as reactions occurring more than 24 hours after transfusion.

  • Delayed hemolytic transfusion reaction (DHTR)
  • Delayed serologic transfusion reaction
  • Alloimmunization
  • TA-GVHD
  • Post-transfusion purpura (PTP)
  • IVIG-related hemolysis
  • Transfusion-transmitted infections

Common symptom-based differential diagnosis

Presentation Important considerations
Fever / rigors FNHTR, AHTR, septic transfusion reaction, TRALI, unrelated fever
Hypotension Anaphylaxis, AHTR, septic shock, TRALI, hypotensive transfusion reaction
Dyspnea / hypoxia TRALI, TACO, TAD, anaphylaxis with bronchospasm
Urticaria / pruritus Allergic reaction, anaphylaxis
Red or dark urine AHTR, hemolysis

Selected Major Reactions

Febrile non-hemolytic transfusion reaction (FNHTR)

FNHTR is characterized by fever, usually defined as a temperature rise of more than 1°C and a temperature above 38°C, during transfusion or within 4 hours after transfusion. Chills or rigors may also qualify even when fever is not prominent.

Management

  • If fever is isolated and the patient is otherwise stable, cautious restart may be considered after assessment.
  • Acetaminophen may be given for symptom control.
  • If there are additional concerning features, investigate for hemolysis or sepsis and do not restart casually.

Transfusion-associated circulatory overload (TACO)

TACO presents with respiratory distress and/or pulmonary edema related to volume overload, generally within 12 hours of transfusion.

Typical features

  • Dyspnea, orthopnea, cough
  • Hypertension
  • Pulmonary edema
  • Elevated JVP
  • Evidence of positive fluid balance
  • Raised BNP may support the diagnosis

Risk factors

  • Cardiac dysfunction, including CHF or recent MI
  • Renal dysfunction
  • Older age or very young age
  • Baseline volume overload or positive fluid balance

Management and prevention

  • Stop transfusion
  • Provide oxygen and diuretics as needed
  • Consider chest imaging
  • For future transfusions, use slower rates, split units, single-unit strategy where appropriate, and consider pre-diuresis

Transfusion-related acute lung injury (TRALI)

TRALI is an acute lung injury occurring during or within 6 hours of transfusion, with hypoxemia and bilateral pulmonary edema not primarily explained by circulatory overload.

Clinical criteria

  • Acute onset during or within 6 hours of transfusion
  • Hypoxemia
  • Bilateral pulmonary infiltrates / edema on imaging
  • No evidence that left atrial hypertension is the main cause

Typical clinical clues

  • More severe hypoxia
  • Hypotension may occur
  • Fever may occur
  • Transient leukopenia may be seen
  • Poor response to diuretics

Pathophysiology

TRALI is commonly explained by a two-event model: a susceptible recipient state with neutrophil priming, followed by transfusion-related donor antibodies or biologic mediators that activate neutrophils and injure pulmonary endothelium.

Management

  • Supportive care
  • Oxygen and ventilatory support as needed
  • Diuretics are generally not helpful unless fluid overload is also present

TRALI versus TACO

Feature TRALI TACO
Timing Usually during or within 6 hours Usually during or within 12 hours
Blood pressure Often normal or low Often elevated
Fever May be present Usually absent
Fluid overload signs Usually absent Usually present
BNP Not typically the main discriminator Often increased
Response to diuretics Poor Usually favorable

Acute hemolytic transfusion reaction (AHTR)

AHTR is usually caused by pre-formed recipient antibodies, most often due to ABO incompatibility, and may become apparent within minutes of starting the transfusion.

Typical features

  • Fever and chills
  • Back pain or pain at the IV site
  • Dyspnea, agitation, or sense of impending doom
  • Hypotension
  • Hemoglobinuria
  • DIC in severe cases

Management

  • Stop transfusion and maintain IV access
  • Urgent blood bank notification and reaction workup
  • Supportive care with IV fluids and hemodynamic support
  • Manage renal injury risk and DIC if present
Most ABO-related AHTRs are caused by human error, including wrong blood in tube, identification errors, or incorrect component transfused.

Allergic and anaphylactic reactions

Allergic transfusion reactions range from mild urticaria to severe anaphylaxis with airway involvement, bronchospasm, or hypotension.

Typical features

  • Mild: hives, pruritus, rash, flushing
  • Severe: facial or airway edema, wheeze, bronchospasm, GI symptoms, hypotension
  • Usually within the first hour of transfusion

Management

  • Interrupt transfusion
  • Treat symptoms appropriately
  • For mild isolated urticaria, antihistamines may be sufficient and cautious restart may be considered if symptoms resolve
  • For anaphylaxis, do not restart; treat urgently according to severity

Hypotensive transfusion reaction

This reaction is defined by a drop in systolic blood pressure of more than 30 mmHg with systolic pressure falling below 80 mmHg, often related to bradykinin-mediated mechanisms.

Associated factors

  • ACE inhibitor therapy
  • Negatively charged bedside filters / leukoreduction filters
  • Rapid infusion
  • Blood warmers in some contexts

Differential diagnosis

  • Anaphylaxis
  • AHTR
  • Septic transfusion reaction
  • TRALI

Delayed hemolytic transfusion reaction (DHTR)

DHTR results from an anamnestic alloantibody response, typically when a previously formed antibody is no longer detectable pre-transfusion but reappears after exposure to the antigen.

Typical timing and features

  • Usually 7 to 14 days after transfusion
  • Unexpected anemia
  • Jaundice
  • May be asymptomatic

Workup

  • CBC and reticulocyte count
  • LDH, bilirubin, smear review
  • Repeat antibody screen
  • DAT, sometimes mixed-field
  • Eluate and further antibody investigation where indicated

Hyperhemolysis

Hyperhemolysis may occur as an acute or delayed phenomenon, most commonly in sickle cell disease, with destruction of both donor and autologous red cells. Reticulocytopenia may be seen, and further transfusion can worsen the process.

TA-GVHD

Transfusion-associated graft-versus-host disease is a delayed, often fatal reaction caused by viable donor lymphocytes engrafting and attacking recipient tissues.

Typical presentation

  • Usually 8 to 14 days after transfusion
  • Fever
  • Rash
  • Liver dysfunction
  • Pancytopenia / marrow aplasia
  • Diarrhea and multisystem failure may occur

Patients at risk

  • Congenital or acquired T-cell immunodeficiency
  • Hodgkin lymphoma
  • Stem cell transplant recipients
  • Recipients of donations from biologic relatives
  • Recipients of HLA-selected products
  • Patients receiving certain immunosuppressive therapies
Prevention is the key principle: use irradiated components when indicated.

Post-transfusion purpura (PTP)

PTP is a delayed immune thrombocytopenic reaction, classically 5 to 10 days after transfusion, usually associated with anti-HPA-1a and characterized by severe thrombocytopenia and bleeding risk.

Septic transfusion reaction

Septic reactions should be suspected in patients with fever, rigors, hypotension, shock, nausea, vomiting, or rapid clinical deterioration during or shortly after transfusion.

Common bacterial sources

  • Undiagnosed donor bacteremia
  • Donor skin contamination during collection
  • Contamination during processing or manipulation

Typical organisms

  • Gram positive: Staphylococcus aureus, Staphylococcus epidermidis, Bacillus cereus
  • Gram negative: Serratia, Klebsiella, Yersinia, Pseudomonas, Escherichia coli

Reporting and Hemovigilance

Definition

An adverse transfusion reaction is an unintended and undesirable response to a blood component or plasma protein product that is definitely, probably, or possibly related to transfusion.

Canadian reporting pathways

Body Typical reporting role
TTISS / TTISS-ON Hemovigilance reporting of serious adverse transfusion reactions; Ontario coordination through TTISS-ON
Canadian Blood Services (CBS) Report suspected component quality or safety issues, including bacterial contamination, mislabelling, and TRALI / possible TRALI
Health Canada Mandatory reporting of serious or fatal adverse reactions under regulatory requirements
Manufacturer of plasma derivatives Report reactions related to plasma protein products such as IVIG and factor concentrates

Reporting deadlines

  • Fatal adverse transfusion reaction: within 24 hours to Health Canada
  • Serious non-fatal adverse transfusion reaction: within 15 days to Health Canada
  • TTISS-ON reporting: typically within 3 months in Ontario

Examples that typically require urgent external reporting

  • TRALI / possible TRALI
  • Severe allergic / anaphylactic reactions
  • Bacterial contamination / septic reaction
  • Fatal or life-threatening reactions
  • Suspected component quality or labelling issue
