Mediastinal Lymphoma Diagnostic Reference

A practical quick-reference page focused on the main mediastinal lymphoma differential: classic Hodgkin lymphoma (CHL), primary mediastinal large B-cell lymphoma (PMBL), and mediastinal gray zone lymphoma (MGZL), with brief notes on DLBCL and NLPHL.

Overview

The principal lymphomas presenting as mediastinal masses are:

Most common Hodgkin-type

Classic Hodgkin lymphoma

Usually the nodular sclerosis subtype when mediastinal.

Major B-cell differential

Primary mediastinal large B-cell lymphoma

Distinct clinicopathologic entity with overlap with CHL.

Overlap category

Mediastinal gray zone lymphoma

Intermediate features between CHL and PMBL.

Less commonly, the mediastinum may be involved by DLBCL or rarely NLPHL, but the most important routine diagnostic problem is resolving the CHL–PMBL–MGZL spectrum.

Practical framing: when a biopsy is from a bulky anterior mediastinal mass in a young patient, first ask whether the case best fits CHL, PMBL, or MGZL before broadening the differential.

Morphology

Classic Hodgkin lymphoma (CHL)

  • Lacunar-type Hodgkin/Reed–Sternberg cells common in nodular sclerosis CHL
  • Broad fibrous bands compartmentalizing nodules
  • Polymorphous inflammatory background
  • Eosinophils and plasma cells often present

Primary mediastinal large B-cell lymphoma (PMBL)

  • Sheets of large cells, often with pale or clear cytoplasm
  • Compartmentalizing or alveolar fibrosis
  • Inflammatory background usually less prominent than in CHL
  • May contain lacunar-like cells and mimic CHL

Mediastinal gray zone lymphoma (MGZL)

  • Mixed or transitional morphology between CHL and PMBL
  • Large pleomorphic cells or Hodgkin-like cells
  • Often sheet-like growth with variable inflammatory milieu
  • Diagnosis is integrative, not purely morphologic

Characteristic Immunophenotypes

Classic Hodgkin lymphoma

CD30 strong
CD15 positive
PAX5 weak
CD20 weak or negative
CD45 negative
BOB1/OCT2 usually negative

The large cells show an attenuated B-cell program. Weak PAX5 is one of the most useful practical clues.

Primary mediastinal large B-cell lymphoma

CD20 strong
PAX5 strong
CD23 positive
CD30 weak or variable
CD45 positive
BOB1/OCT2 positive

PMBL retains a B-cell phenotype and often shows a characteristic mediastinal clinical setting and fibrosis.

Mediastinal gray zone lymphoma

CD20 strong
CD30 strong
PAX5 moderate to strong
CD15 variable
CD45 usually positive

MGZL is favored when the case shows morphologic overlap plus a hybrid immunophenotype, such as CHL-like morphology with unexpectedly strong B-cell marker expression.

NLPHL Does not belong to the CHL–PMBL mediastinal overlap spectrum

Diagnostic Algorithm

  1. Recognize the setting: young patient with an anterior mediastinal mass strongly raises CHL, PMBL, or MGZL.
  2. Assess morphology: inflammatory background and classic lacunar cells favor CHL; sheet-like large B cells with fibrosis favor PMBL; mixed or contradictory features raise MGZL.
  3. Start with the core panel: CD20, PAX5, CD30, CD15, CD45.
  4. Interpret PAX5 intensity: weak favors CHL; strong supports PMBL or MGZL.
  5. Evaluate B-cell marker preservation: strong CD20 and CD45 support PMBL/MGZL over CHL.
  6. Add discriminatory markers: CD23, BOB1, OCT2, MUM1, PD-L1, and EBER as needed.
  7. Call MGZL cautiously: reserve for cases with genuine overlap, not merely imperfect staining or limited biopsy artifact.

Key Diagnostic Pearls

  • Weak PAX5 is one of the most useful day-to-day clues favoring CHL.
  • CD23 positivity strongly supports PMBL in the right clinical and morphologic setting.
  • Strong CD20 plus strong CD30 in a mediastinal tumor should raise MGZL.
  • PMBL can mimic nodular sclerosis CHL because both may show fibrosis and lacunar-like cells.
  • Small biopsies can be misleading; architecture is often underrepresented, so correlation with imaging and clinical setting matters.
  • Not every unusual mediastinal biopsy with partial overlap is MGZL; the label should be used deliberately.
